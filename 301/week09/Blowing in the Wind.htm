2. Time-series analysis of wind speeds

  • a) Pick out three buoys from someplace in U.S. waters that is of interest to you, and use your package to load their data (Note: not all of the buoys listed in the master table have downloadable data for 2012, so you may encounter errors!).
  • b) Merge the three time series of wind speeds into a single zoo object and aggregate the merged time-series by day to obtain a mean daily wind-speed. Plot the mean daily wind speed.
  • c) Take one of the three wind stations as a response variable (Y1), and the other two as explanatory variables (Y2, Y3). (Note: This setup is quite relevant for testing the ability to predict winds in a specific location based on data from meteorological stations nearby). Break up the one year of data into a “training” and “predicting” data set as follows:
Y.train <- na.omit(Y[1:300])
Y.predict <- na.omit(Y[300:366])
  • d) Use gls() to fit a model that includes the main and interaction effects of the two covariates (Y2 * Y3), but no autocorrelation structure; one model that includes a first order autocorrelation structure AR(1) and one that includes an AR(2) autocorrelation structure for the residuals. Evaluate the predictions of these models for the testing subset of the data (e.g.~via a correlation coefficient). Which performs better? Which has the lowest AIC?

3. Markov-chain analysis of wind direction

  • a) Pick one buoy, and report the proportion of time that the wind blows North, East, South and West, respectively. What direction is the dominant wind direction for your buoy? Under the assumption that wind direction is an independent process, what would the expected duration of wind blowing consecutively in that direction be? (Hint: recall - and look up - the geometric distribution.)
  • b) Obtain (and report) an estimated 10-minute Markov transition matrix M for wind directions. According to this matrix, how long can one expect the wind to blow consecutively in the dominant wind direction?
  • c) Compare your two estimates to the actual mean duration of wind blowing in a particular direction. (Hint: use the rle() function to obtain run lengths.)
  • d) EXTRA CREDIT: Discuss how you could use run lengths as a statistic to perform the following hypothesis tests on Markov chains: (i) assessing independence, (ii) comparing two Markov chains (e.g. from neighboring buoys), (iii) determining the order of a Markov chain.
